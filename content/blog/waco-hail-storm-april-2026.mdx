## What Does Central Texas Hail Do to Your Roof?

### Granule Loss: The Silent Killer

Asphalt shingles are coated with mineral granules that serve three critical functions:
1. **UV protection** — shields the asphalt base from Texas sun degradation
2. **Fire resistance** — forms a barrier against ember spread
3. **Water shedding** — helps channel rain off the surface

When hail strikes a shingle at 50–70 mph terminal velocity, it **blasts granules off** in a circular impact pattern. From the ground, your roof may look fine. But in your gutters, you'll find dark, sandy material — that's your roof's protective layer washing away.

**Check your gutters today.** Significant granule accumulation after a hail event is one of the strongest forms of claim evidence.

### Bruising and Mat Fractures

Beneath the granules is an asphalt-fiberglass mat. Direct hail impact — even from 3/4" stones — can **fracture that mat without breaking through** to the surface. This "functional loss" means the shingle is no longer performing as designed, even if it looks intact from 20 feet below.

Insurance adjusters use a chalk grid test and physical pressure to identify mat fractures. An experienced roofing contractor will document the same way.

### Seal Strip Damage

Modern architectural shingles have a self-sealing adhesive strip that bonds layers together and prevents wind uplift. Repeated hail impacts can **break that bond**, leaving your shingles vulnerable to blow-off in the next storm.

### Metal Component Dents

Hail leaves unmistakable evidence on soft metal surfaces: AC condenser fins, gutter edges, ridge cap flashing, pipe boots, and chimney caps. These dents are often more visible than shingle damage and serve as critical supporting evidence for your insurance claim.

## How to Document Your Hail Damage Today

The clock is running. Here's the documentation sequence that maximizes your claim:

### Step 1: Check Your Gutters and Downspouts

Go outside right now and inspect:
- **Granule accumulation** in gutters — photograph any significant buildup
- **Dented gutter edges** — fresh dents with no oxidation mean recent impact
- **Downspout dents** — same verification on vertical surfaces

Gutter dents are among the strongest insurance claim evidence because they are verifiable, dateable, and impossible to fabricate.

### Step 2: Inspect Soft Metal Surfaces

Before getting on the roof:
- **AC condenser fins** — new dents without oxidation indicate this storm
- **Garage door panels** — fresh dings
- **Window screens and frames** — hail tears screen mesh and dents aluminum frames
- **Mailbox top** — surprisingly useful hail evidence
- **Painted fence caps and deck rails**

Photograph everything with timestamps. These items confirm hail size and intensity independently of the roof.

### Step 3: Do a Ground-Level Roof Survey

**Do not get on the roof yourself.** Walk all four sides and look for:
- Missing shingles or obvious surface damage
- Dark circular impact marks or missing granule zones (use binoculars or phone zoom)
- Ridge cap damage — peaks take direct hits and are often the most visible
- Chimney flashing dents

### Step 4: Record a Video Walkthrough

Use your phone to record a continuous video of your entire property, narrating what you see. Video timestamps are automatic. Combined with the official NWS storm record, this creates a clear timeline for your adjuster.

### Step 5: Document Neighbor Damage

If neighboring homes show visible damage, photograph from the street. Widespread damage across multiple homes in a cluster strengthens every individual claim.

### 3. Know Your Policy Type: ACV vs. RCV

- **ACV (Actual Cash Value):** Pays depreciated roof value. A 15-year-old roof on an ACV policy may receive minimal payment.
- **RCV (Replacement Cost Value):** Pays to replace your roof with like kind and quality. This is what you want.

Check your declarations page before the adjuster arrives. If you're on ACV and your roof is older than 10 years, ask your agent about upgrade options now.

### 4. Beware of Storm Chasers

After a major hail event in Waco, out-of-town contractors flood neighborhoods. Many ask you to sign an **Assignment of Benefits (AOB)** or "direction to pay" form — these transfer your insurance rights to the contractor, removing your ability to negotiate or dispute the claim.

## Why the Waco Area Gets Hit So Hard

Waco's geographic location is, frankly, unlucky when it comes to severe weather. The city sits at the convergence of three key factors:

**1. Storm Track Alignment**
Severe thunderstorms that develop over the Concho Valley and Edwards Plateau track northeast along a corridor that passes almost directly over Waco before continuing toward Dallas/Fort Worth. This gives McLennan County some of the state's highest frequency of large hail.

**2. Flat Terrain**
Unlike Hill Country areas with natural topographic barriers, the Brazos River plains around Waco offer no terrain-based protection from approaching storms. Winds and hail carry full force across the metro area.

**3. The Dry Line**
Texas's infamous dry line — the boundary between moist Gulf air and dry continental air — frequently sets up near the I-35 corridor. Where the dry line stalls or oscillates, supercell thunderstorms with large hail are most likely.

This isn't speculation — it's documented in NOAA's historical hail data. McLennan County property owners should treat impact-resistant roofing as an investment necessity, not a luxury.

## Frequently Asked Questions: Waco Hail Damage

**Q: My roof looks fine from the street. Do I still need an inspection?**
A: Yes. The most expensive hail damage — bruised mats, granule loss, broken seal strips — is invisible from street level. A professional inspection with a chalk grid test takes 45 minutes and is free. The cost of a missed claim can be $15,000–$30,000.

**Q: How long do I have to file a claim?**
A: Texas law requires reporting "as soon as practicable." Your policy may also have a specific time limit (often 1–2 years). However, insurers use weather records to verify storm dates — the official NWS record supports your claim as long as you act reasonably promptly. Don't wait months.

**Q: The insurance adjuster said I have no damage. What do I do?**
A: Request a re-inspection with your contractor present, or have your contractor create a formal supplemental estimate. You have the right to dispute an adjuster's findings. A public adjuster or your contractor can advocate for you.

**Q: Can I just patch the damaged areas?**
A: Partial repairs are possible for minor localized damage. However, if hail has caused widespread granule loss across the roof surface, a full replacement is typically required — and what your insurer should cover under an RCV policy.

**Q: Should I get impact-resistant shingles if I'm replacing my roof?**
A: In McLennan County, the answer is almost always yes. The insurance discount ROI is compelling, and the protection through future hail events is significant. Ask us for a side-by-side comparison during your inspection.
