## What Does 1-Inch Hail Do to Your Roof?

One inch. That's about the size of a **quarter coin** — and it causes more damage than most homeowners realize. Here's why:

### Granule Loss: The Silent Killer

Asphalt shingles are coated with granules (crushed mineral aggregate) that do three critical jobs:
1. **UV protection** — granules block the sun from degrading the asphalt below
2. **Fire resistance** — they form a protective barrier
3. **Water shedding** — they help channel water off the surface

When 1" hail strikes a shingle at 50–70 mph (typical terminal velocity), it **blasts granules off** in a circular impact pattern. You may not see visible cracks from the ground — but those granule-loss zones accelerate aging by **5–10 years per impact**.

Look in your gutters: if they're full of dark, sand-like granules after Saturday's storm, that's your roof's protective layer washing away.

### Bruising and Mat Fractures

Beneath those granules is an asphalt-fiberglass mat. A direct 1" hail strike can **fracture or bruise that mat** without breaking through the surface. This is called a "functional loss" — the shingle is no longer performing as designed, even if it looks intact from 20 feet below.

Insurance adjusters are trained to test for this with a chalk grid and physical pressure. An experienced roofing contractor will identify it the same way.

### Seal Strip Damage

Modern shingles have a self-sealing adhesive strip that bonds shingles together and prevents wind uplift. High-velocity hail impact — especially repeated strikes — can **break that seal**, leaving shingles vulnerable to blow-off in the next storm.

### What 1" Hail Does NOT Do (Usually)

To set expectations honestly: 1-inch hail typically **won't punch visible holes** through standard 3-tab or architectural shingles. It also may not crack metal flashing on first impact. The damage is often **cumulative and invisible** from street level — which is exactly why insurance companies send trained adjusters, and why you need a contractor on your side who documents it correctly.

## How to Document Your Hail Damage (Do This Today)

The clock is running. Most Texas homeowner insurance policies require you to report damage **within a reasonable time** — and adjusters use weather data to verify storm dates. The April 18 NWS report is your timestamp. Here's how to document before you call your insurer:

### Step 1: Check Your Gutters and Downspouts

Go outside right now and look in your gutters. Take a photo of:
- **Granule accumulation** (dark, sand-like material)
- **Dented gutter edges** — hail frequently dents aluminum gutters, creating visible proof
- **Downspout dents** — same evidence on vertical surfaces

Gutter dents are among the strongest evidence in an insurance claim because they're **verifiable, dateable, and impossible to fake**.

### Step 2: Check Soft Metal Surfaces

Before getting on the roof, inspect:
- **AC condenser fins** — look for fresh dents (pre-existing dents will have oxidation; new ones won't)
- **Garage door panels** — fresh dings without paint flaking
- **Window screens and frames** — hail tears screen mesh and dents aluminum frames
- **Mailbox top** — surprisingly useful hail evidence
- **Painted wood fence caps or deck rails**

Photograph everything with your phone. These are "collateral confirmation" items adjusters use to verify hail size and intensity.

### Step 3: Do a Ground-Level Roof Survey

**Do not get on the roof yourself.** This is important — both for your safety and your claim. Instead, from the ground:

- **Walk all four sides** of the house and look for missing shingles or obvious damage
- **Use binoculars or zoom in with your phone camera** to look for dark circular impact marks or missing granule areas
- Look at **ridge caps** (the peak of your roof) — these take direct hits and are often the most visually obvious
- Check your **chimney flashing and pipe boots** — metal components show dents clearly

### Step 4: Video Walkthrough

Record a continuous video walkthrough of your entire property, narrating what you see: *"This is my backyard at [address], April 20, 2026 — you can see dents in the AC unit cover here, and granules in this gutter section..."*

Video timestamps are timestamped by your phone automatically. This video, combined with the official April 18 NWS storm report, creates a clear timeline for your adjuster.

### Step 5: Document Neighbors' Damage

If your neighbors have visible damage (dents, missing shingles, debris), photograph their property from the street. Widespread damage across multiple homes in a cluster strengthens every individual claim.

## Filing Your Insurance Claim: Texas-Specific Steps

### 1. Call Your Insurance Company (Or File Online) Within 48–72 Hours

You've already documented. Now call and open the claim. Key things to say:
- *"I experienced hail damage on April 18, 2026 from a confirmed NWS storm in Williamson County, TX."*
- Request an adjuster inspection
- Ask for your claim number immediately

### 2. Get a Contractor Inspection BEFORE the Adjuster Arrives

This is the step most homeowners skip — and it costs them thousands. A licensed roofing contractor who inspects your roof **before** the adjuster creates an independent damage report. ### 3. Understand Your ACV vs. RCV Policy

Texas homeowners policies typically fall into two categories:
- **ACV (Actual Cash Value):** Pays depreciated value of your roof. A 15-year-old roof might pay very little.
- **RCV (Replacement Cost Value):** Pays to replace your roof with like kind and quality. This is what you want.

Check your declarations page before the adjuster arrives. If you're on ACV and your roof is older, you may have upgrade options — ask your agent.

### 4. Do Not Let Storm Chasers Sign Your Rights Away

After a major hail event, out-of-town "storm chasers" flood neighborhoods offering free inspections and asking you to sign an **Assignment of Benefits (AOB)** or "direction to pay" form. These contracts transfer your insurance rights to the contractor — removing your ability to negotiate or dispute the claim.

## Why Act in the Next 7–10 Days?

1. **Your policy's "timely reporting" clause.** Texas courts have upheld claim denials when homeowners waited months to report. The official April 18 storm report is on the record today.

2. **Contractor availability.** After a confirmed hail event, local roofers fill up fast. The worst outcome is waiting 6 weeks, getting a second storm, and having an adjuster argue the damage is from the newer event.

3. **Water intrusion.** Cracked or granule-depleted shingles allow moisture to enter roof decking. 30 days of Texas spring rain accelerates rot, mold, and structural damage — all of which may **not** be covered once they're attributed to delayed maintenance rather than storm damage.
