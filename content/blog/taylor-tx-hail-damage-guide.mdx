## Step 1: Don't Go On Your Roof

It sounds obvious, but every post-storm period brings calls from homeowners who slipped or fell doing their own inspection. Hail-damaged shingles lose granules, becoming slick surfaces. Wet or damaged decking can be unstable underfoot.

Leave the roof to a licensed professional. What you *can* safely do from the ground:

- Walk your property perimeter and photograph any visible damage to shingles, gutters, fascia, and downspouts
- Check your AC unit condenser fins — they dent from hail and are a reliable visible indicator of event severity
- Examine soft metal surfaces: chimney caps, vent covers, gutters, and any exposed flashing — dents here confirm hail size and intensity
- Note any dented or cracked window screens or sill trim

Photograph everything with timestamps. These ground-level photos are supporting evidence for your claim.

## Step 3: File Your Claim Promptly — Texas Has a 1-Year Limit

Texas Insurance Code gives homeowners **one year from the date of a storm** to file a claim for hail or wind damage. That window sounds long, but it closes fast if you're waiting to "see if it gets worse" or dealing with other post-storm priorities.

**What you need to file:**
- Your policy number and insurance carrier contact
- Approximate date of the storm event (check local news records or the National Weather Service storm database for the date)
- Your contractor's written inspection report (ideally already in hand)
- Photos of damage from your ground-level walkthrough

File over the phone or through your carrier's app. Get a claim number and document the date you filed — this timestamp matters if there's ever a dispute about timeliness.

## Step 4: The Adjuster Walkthrough — What to Expect

Your carrier will schedule a field adjuster to inspect the property. A few things to know:

**Have your contractor present.** This is non-negotiable if you want a fair outcome. Your contractor and the adjuster walking the roof together, side by side, produces significantly better results than an adjuster walking alone and a contractor reviewing their findings after the fact.

**Common adjuster disputes on Taylor roofs:**
- *Granule loss without cracking:* Adjusters sometimes argue granule loss doesn't warrant replacement. A contractor with hail impact documentation can counter this — granule loss accelerates aging and is covered under most policies after a qualifying event.
- *"Functional damage" vs. "cosmetic damage":* Texas policies vary on cosmetic exclusions. Know what your policy says before the walkthrough.
- *Existing wear vs. storm damage:* Adjusters may try to attribute damage to normal wear. Your contractor's photos documenting the impact pattern help distinguish storm damage from aging.

**If the adjuster's scope is too low:** You have the right to request a reinspection and, if needed, invoke your policy's appraisal clause (which brings in a neutral third-party appraiser). Don't sign a settlement you believe is inadequate — get a second contractor opinion first.

## Step 5: Understand Your Payout — ACV vs. RCV

Texas homeowners policies pay out in one of two ways:

**Actual Cash Value (ACV):** What your roof is worth today, accounting for depreciation. A 15-year-old roof gets significantly less than a new one. You receive ACV at initial payout.

**Replacement Cost Value (RCV):** The full cost to replace your roof with comparable materials at today's prices — no depreciation deducted. Most policies have RCV coverage, but you typically receive ACV first, then the "recoverable depreciation" after the work is completed and you submit proof.

**What this means practically:**
1. You receive the ACV check — often enough to get started
2. Work gets completed by a licensed contractor
3. You submit the final invoice to your carrier
4. Carrier releases the depreciation holdback (recoverable depreciation)

## What Hail Does to Taylor Roofs — The Technical Reality

Understanding what hail actually does to a roof helps you evaluate damage claims and contractor recommendations.

### Asphalt and Architectural Shingles

Hail impacts compress the granule layer into the fiberglass mat, loosening granule adhesion and exposing the underlying asphalt to UV radiation. The damage isn't always immediately visible from below — impact sites are often dime-to-quarter-sized circular areas where granules are depressed or missing.

Over time, these impact sites:
- Accelerate UV degradation of the asphalt
- Create micro-cracking in the fiberglass mat
- Become entry points for moisture infiltration
- Reduce the shingle's rated lifespan by 5–10+ years depending on severity

### Large Hail (1.5"+)

Golf-ball-sized and larger hail (which Taylor has seen in documented events) can:
- Crack ridge caps outright
- Punch through older 3-tab shingles
- Dent and deform metal flashing at penetrations
- Crack pipe boots if they've become brittle from age and heat

### What's Covered vs. What's Not

Standard homeowners policies cover sudden storm damage. They typically don't cover:
- Normal wear and aging
- Cosmetic damage only (if your policy has a cosmetic exclusion rider — check your declarations page)
- Pre-existing damage from prior events

If you have an older roof (15+ years) and there are multiple storm events in the history, your claim may be complicated by questions of "which event caused this?" — which is another reason a contractor with thorough documentation is valuable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does a hail damage claim take in Texas?**
Most straightforward claims resolve in 4–8 weeks from the date of filing. Complex claims with disputes can take 3–6 months if appraisal is invoked.

**Can I do repairs before the adjuster comes?**
Emergency repairs to prevent further damage (tarping an active leak, for example) are generally covered and advisable. Do not do permanent repairs before the adjuster inspection — this limits the carrier's ability to assess and may reduce your payout.

**What if my claim is denied?**
You can dispute a denial. Request a written denial letter with the specific policy language the carrier is relying on. Have a second contractor review the denial rationale. If you believe the denial is improper, you can file a complaint with the Texas Department of Insurance or invoke your policy's appraisal process.

**Does filing a claim raise my rates?**
Possibly — a storm claim is generally a "no-fault" event but carriers do factor claim history into renewal pricing. If the damage is minor, it may be worth comparing the repair cost to your deductible before filing. For significant damage, the claim is almost always worth it.
