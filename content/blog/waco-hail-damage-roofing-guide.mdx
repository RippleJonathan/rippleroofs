## Why Waco Gets Hit So Hard

McLennan County's severe weather frequency isn't bad luck — it's geography.

### The Central Texas Storm Track

Waco sits almost perfectly along the primary severe weather corridor that tracks from the Texas Hill Country and Edwards Plateau northeast toward Dallas/Fort Worth. When supercell thunderstorms develop over the Concho Valley or Rolling Plains, Waco is directly in their path 4–6 hours later.

### The Dry Line Factor

Texas's infamous "dry line" — the boundary between moist Gulf of Mexico air advancing from the southeast and dry continental air from the west — frequently sets up along the I-35 corridor. When the dry line stalls near Waco and conditions are favorable, supercell storms capable of producing baseball-sized hail develop rapidly.

### Flat Terrain, No Barriers

Unlike communities in the Hill Country that benefit from topographic disruption of storm systems, the Brazos River plains around Waco offer no natural barriers. Hail and wind arrive at full force.

**The result:** NOAA data shows McLennan County averaging 4–7 significant hail events annually, with multiple events per year exceeding 1 inch in diameter. Over the course of a 20–30 year roof lifespan, the cumulative damage potential is enormous.

## Hail Size and What It Does to Your Roof

Understanding hail size helps you understand the claim process:

| Hail Size | Common Reference | Roof Impact |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| < 1/2" | Pea | Minimal — granule displacement, generally not a functional loss |
| 3/4" | Dime / Penny | Granule loss begins, seal strip damage possible |
| 1" | Quarter | Functional loss likely — granule loss, possible mat bruising |
| 1.25" | Half dollar | Significant functional loss, possible mat fracture |
| 1.5" | Golf ball | Severe damage — replacement typically required |
| 1.75"+ | Baseball+ | Catastrophic damage — immediate replacement needed |

McLennan County has recorded hail exceeding 3 inches in diameter in recent years. Even "modest" 1-inch events cause damage that compounds over time.

## The Four Types of Hail Damage

### 1. Granule Loss

Asphalt shingles are covered with mineral granules that:
- Block UV radiation from breaking down the asphalt base
- Provide fire resistance
- Help channel water off the surface

Hail impacts blast granules off in circular patterns. Individual strikes may be subtle, but when multiplied across thousands of impacts per storm, the cumulative granule loss significantly shortens roof life.

**How to spot it:** Look in your gutters after any significant storm. Dark, sand-like granule accumulation is your first indication of hail impact.

### 2. Asphalt Mat Bruising

The asphalt-fiberglass mat beneath the granules is the structural core of each shingle. Direct hail impact can fracture this mat without creating a visible surface crack. Called a "functional loss," the shingle is no longer performing as engineered even if it appears intact from below.

**How to spot it:** A contractor uses a chalk grid test — marking 10x10-foot sections, then physically testing each impact mark with thumb pressure. Functional loss shows as a soft, spongy impact zone versus the firm surface of undamaged areas.

**Why it matters:** Insurers recognize functional loss as a legitimate basis for replacement. This is often the most important documentation item in a McLennan County hail claim.

### 3. Seal Strip Failure

Modern architectural shingles have a self-sealing adhesive strip that thermally bonds layers together. This seal prevents wind uplift and water infiltration at the shingle edges.

Repeated hail impacts — particularly from large stones — can break these seals. Once broken, shingles are vulnerable to blow-off in subsequent wind events and to water infiltration at the unsealed edge.

### 4. Flashing and Component Damage

Metal components around your roof — chimney flashing, pipe boot covers, valley metal, ridge vent caps — show hail damage visually and clearly. Dents in these materials:
- Confirm hail occurred on your specific property
- Support the date of loss from the NWS storm record
- Strengthen the overall claim for shingle replacement

AC condensing units, gutters, and garage doors serve the same evidentiary purpose.

## Inspecting Your Roof After a Waco Hail Storm

### What You Can Do Yourself (Ground Level Only)

**Safety first: Do not get on the roof.** Wet shingles are extremely slippery, and post-storm conditions create fall risk. Everything you need to document at the homeowner level can be done from the ground.

**Gutter Inspection:**
- Walk the perimeter of your home and inspect all gutters
- Look for granule accumulation (dark, sand-like material)
- Photograph any gutter and downspout dents
- Check where downspouts discharge for granule buildup

**Soft Metal Survey:**
- Inspect AC unit fins for fresh dents (no oxidation = recent)
- Check garage door panels for dings
- Look at window screen frames and window sills
- Check mailbox top, fence cap rails, deck rails

**Ground-Level Roof Observation:**
- Walk all four sides of the house
- Use phone camera zoom or binoculars to inspect ridge caps
- Look for missing shingles or visible dark impact zones
- Check chimney cap and exposed metal flashing

**Record Everything:**
Use your phone to record a timestamped video walkthrough of your entire property, narrating what you observe. This video, combined with the official NWS storm report for McLennan County, creates a powerful documentation record.

### What a Professional Inspection Adds

A licensed roofing contractor's inspection goes significantly further:
- **Chalk grid test** — systematic functional loss identification
- **Seal strip testing** — physical inspection of bond integrity on representative shingles
- **Ridge and valley examination** — areas most exposed to direct impact
- **Flashing documentation** — photographs of all metal component damage
- **Core samples** — in some cases, core sampling confirms impact depth
- **Written damage report** — formal documentation suitable for insurance submission

**Get the professional inspection before the insurance adjuster visits.** An independent contractor report in hand before the adjuster's visit means you have documentation to supplement any items they miss — and adjusters do miss items, especially in high-volume storm response periods.

## Insurance Claims for McLennan County Hail Damage

### Texas Homeowner Policy Basics

Texas homeowner insurance policies vary significantly. Before your adjuster visits, locate your policy and confirm:

**Coverage Type:**
- *RCV (Replacement Cost Value)* — pays to replace your roof with equivalent materials, regardless of age. This is the preferred coverage.
- *ACV (Actual Cash Value)* — pays depreciated value. A 15-year-old roof on ACV may receive very limited payment.

**Deductible Type:**
- *Wind/Hail Deductible* — many Texas policies have a separate, higher deductible for wind and hail claims, often expressed as a percentage of the home's insured value (e.g., 1–2%). On a $400,000 home, a 1% deductible means $4,000 out of pocket before coverage applies.

**Reporting Timeframe:**
Texas policies typically require reporting "as soon as practicable." Practically, report within 48–72 hours of discovering the damage. The official NWS storm record establishes your date of loss.

### The Claim Process Step by Step

**1. Document first** (see the section above)

**2. Call your insurer and open the claim**
- Provide the storm date and county
- Request a licensed adjuster (not a field inspector)
- Get your claim number

**3. Schedule a contractor inspection before the adjuster**
A formal damage report from your contractor before the adjuster's visit is your single most powerful claim tool. [Call us to schedule](/contact): **(512) 763-5277**

**4. Meet the adjuster with your contractor present if possible**
Two sets of eyes on your roof is always better. Your contractor can point out items in real time that an adjuster might overlook.

**5. Review the adjuster's report carefully**
- Check that all damage categories are documented (granule loss, bruising, metal, gutters)
- Verify the scope of work matches your contractor's assessment
- If items are missed, ask your contractor to file a formal supplement

**6. Select your contractor and execute the replacement**
Once approved, work with a licensed Texas contractor who uses quality materials and provides a workmanship warranty. The insurer typically pays RCV value minus the deductible.

Class 4 is the highest UL 2218 impact resistance rating. Shingles earn this rating by withstanding a 2-inch steel ball dropped from 20 feet without cracking — simulating large hailstone impact. Class 4 shingles are engineered to perform where standard shingles fail.

## Waco's Neighborhoods and Their Roofing Profiles

### Woodway & Hewitt: Premium Suburbs

The most affluent Waco-area communities have premium roofing expectations — and often strict HOA requirements for materials, colors, and contractor qualifications. Class 4 shingles in designer colors (weathered wood, charcoal, aged copper) are the standard upgrade in these neighborhoods.

Typical replacement cost: $18,000–$35,000 for 2,500–4,500 sq ft homes

### Baylor University Area: Historic Homes, Special Considerations

Homes around campus range from 1920s craftsmen to 1960s ranch houses. Many have steeper pitches, unique architectural details, and original wood decking that may require replacement before new shingles go on.

Typical replacement cost: $12,000–$22,000, plus $2,000–$5,000 for decking repairs on older homes

### West Waco & Sanger Heights: Established Neighborhoods

Mid-century homes built between the 1950s and 1980s. Many are at or past original roof replacement age. Hail damage in these areas often reveals compounding issues — original decking, inadequate ventilation, outdated flashings.

Typical replacement cost: $13,000–$21,000 with an additional allowance for decking and ventilation upgrades

### Bellmead / Lacy-Lakeview: Working Communities

Older, affordable housing stock with high utility. Many roofs in this area have deferred maintenance. Post-hail inspections frequently reveal cumulative damage from multiple storms over the years. Insurance claims here need careful documentation to separate new storm damage from pre-existing wear.

Typical replacement cost: $10,000–$17,000

### McGregor and Western McLennan County

Rural and semi-rural properties with larger roof footprints and open-terrain exposure. Hail and wind hit harder in open country with no neighboring structures providing shelter. Impact-resistant materials are especially valuable here.

Typical replacement cost varies widely by home size; metal roofing is also a popular option in rural settings.
